CodementorX – Hiring Dashboard Revamp

CodementorX is a product/service which helps clients hire freelance software developers on-demand. The “hiring dashboard” is for the “clients” to monitor the hiring process and to know what to do next.

(Sadly, CodementorX is now rebranded as Arc.dev, so you can’t see my work in production:( But go check their new look and branding. It’s awesome!)

Year: 2018
Project type: Work project
Duration: 2-3 weeks
Role: Product designer

How CodementorX works

Two biggest pain points for a client to hire a freelance developer are the screening process and annoying hassles that occur during the project. To solve these pain points for clients, CodementorX build a very rigorous vetting process to filter high quality developers for clients, and it serves as a third party to make sure both client and developer will comply with the hiring contract.

Therefore, it’s extremely easy for the clients to hire a freelance developer on CodementorX. The only things the clients have to do is filling out an onboarding form to tell our sales team what their project is about, and then they will get a shortlist, interview candidates, sign a contract, and they’re good to start the project.

Why dashboard revamp

CodementorX’s old hiring dashboard is basically a “chat room”. After the clients sign up and fill out the onboarding form, they will be directed to this chatroom, where they receive the shortlist, interview information, and contract. Basically the entire hiring process happens in the chatroom.

However, as we try to scale our business and our hiring process evolves consequently, our sales team finds that the “chat room” is no longer the most efficient way to communicate with clients and thus doesn’t use it anymore. In the new hiring process, our sales team prefers to contact clients via video/audio call and email.

Project goal

Therefore, the primary goal of this project is to remove the chat room from the dashboard but still keep other features such as shortlist, interview info, etc. In the meantime, as we want to scale our business, we want to evaluate what improvements we can make in the dashboard to streamline the hiring process, or even automate some parts of the process. Lastly, as this is going to be a huge change of our product, we want to make sure we will be able to maintain users’ experience and even improve it.

Research

First, I talk to our sales team to further clarify the new hiring process and determine what exact content I should add or remove from the new hiring dashboard; second, I find 10+ heavy and/or recent users (clients) in our database, and use Fullstory to observe how they actually use our old dashboard. Last but not least, I collect a lot of user feedback from the weekly meetings I have with our sales team and support team. Although these feedback are “second hand”, they are still valuable data points in terms of validating the data I have from other places and helping me better construct the blueprint of the new dashboard.

Synthesize findings

After the research I get some data from observing user behavior and user feedback. I turn them into the affinity note and cluster them to identify patterns and prioritize design strategies:

  • Although we need to remove the “chat room”, we still need to include a feature for clients to reach out to our sales team in the dashboard
  • We have to show the step by step hiring process which provides a better mental model and more transparency. In this way, clients can easily find the information and expect what is next
  • Allow clients to initiate an action, such as schedule interview, send a contract to developers once they decide to hire them, etc. This will help us “automate” the hiring process
  • As we are having more and more “returning clients”, we need to introduce the concept of “hiring request” to the dashboard. In our old dashboard, it’s hard to find the information of individual hiring request if they have more than one, and there’s not a clear way for clients to make a new hiring request

Information architecture

After talking to our sales team, we clarify the standard hiring process as following: 1) before project start: fill out onboarding form, review the candidates shortlist, check candidates’ profile, schedule interviews, interview, leave feedback, and sign contract; 2) after hiring: the clients can monitor the project process. Prototype round 1

Layout exploration

I create three different layouts and evaluate which is the best by the following criteria: 1) is it the easiest way for users to access information; 2) does it look good both in empty case and with full content; 3) are the steps of hiring process clear enough and easy to interact with.

Finally, I choose Option A to proceed because it shows Request and Contract upfront, it will look good in empty case, and it has a progress bar in request detail page. Option B pull all the hiring steps out and put them in the highest level of the dashboard. It’s clear but the screen will look too empty in empty case, and it doesn’t display the hiring process very well. Option C has a similar structure as Option A, but it requires an extra step to click into the request detail page, so this is also a no go.

Interaction detail

After deciding which layout to go with, I make an iteration to further explore what the detail should look like and how users are going to interact with it.

Midway sync up

Knowing the constraint and best development practice

Before diving into the design detail, I will always have a sync up meeting with product manager and developers who are going to implement the new dashboard. In meeting like this, I will go through the prototype that I have at the moment and explain every logic and interaction in the prototype. The product manager will make sure that the new design won’t conflict our business goal or defer the project timeline. On the other hand, the developer will provide feedback in terms of the feasibility of implementation.

Design strategy adjustment

Usually, the result from the sync up meeting will contribute to the iteration later on. In this case, there are two main takeaways that affect my design decisions.

  • First, using Intercome for clients to communicate with sales. Originally, I plan to design a lightweight chat feature to do that, but it requires too many resources and at the moment we are not sure that this is worth the investment. Intercome is already a third party service we use in other places in our product
  • Second, confirm that we separate “Hiring request” and “Contract” is the right direction. Previously, I consider put contract under a hiring request because, in common sense, a contract is a part or a result of a hiring request. However, since in the way our current system is designed, we do not link a contract to a hiring request
  • Third, remove the “blue dot” notification. I planned to use this to notify users what to do next, but this it will require too much effort of implementation

User testing

After the mid way sync up, I use the same prototype to conduct some user testing by which I got the following results:

  • Menu: users feel that the request and contract list (second column) don’t need to take a whole column because they probably won’t have “a lot” of requests or contracts. Also, users can’t focus on the main content immediately because this two-columns-menu takes too much space
  • Progress bar: users don’t feel like it’s a progress bar because the relationship between each step is not obvious enough
  • Main content interaction: the interaction in the shortlist step and the interview step is not intuitive, and user can’t figure out what to do with the UI
  • Main content layout: some users are confused by the two different layouts. They don’t feel they are in the same process

Prototype round 2

Iteration

For the menu, I merge the two-columns-menu into one, and put the request title on top of the progress bar, making them into one nav bar. I also add a background color to the nav bar which provides a stronger hierarchy that can better help users identify the main content and the navbar.

For the main content interaction, the original design is more like a “sub progress” inside each card, and use background color to indicate the status of each “sub step”. Since this is confusing for users, I use a clear status tag to indicate the status and remove the concept of “sub step”.

For the main content layout, I change all the layouts into one column so the content will look more consistent through each step.

Conclusion

What can be done better

I wish I could have a better understanding of our clients. Designers in CodementorX don’t usually conduct thorough user research because of limited resources and time zone issue (the product team is not in the same time zone as most of the clients). Our understanding of users (both clients and developers) is accumulated from the interaction we have with them over the entire time. It works well in most of the time, but I believe that there might be some bias and we could discover more valuable insight by conducting interviews with clients.

What I learned

This project pretty much summarizes my work experience in Codementor (our company name is Codementor, CodementorX is one of the products that I was in charge of). There are two valuable takeaways I got from being a product designer in this company:

  • First, having a “product sense” is equally important as design skills. Design is only one aspect of a product. Developing a product sense means that pay attention to how the company/business works but not just focus on design itself. It helps me think of user experience as a whole not just the experience using the interface.
  • Second, learning the implementation knowledge. Designers do not design in the vaccune and never work alone. By collaborating with our developers, I learned a lot about how our database is design and how the front-end works. Acquiring these knowledge helps me design more efficiently.

Future of CodementorX

You might notice that there are quite a lot of “standalone” pages in CodementorX. This is because of the legacy. CodementorX is built step by step. Over the past few years, we tested different strategies that meet our business goals and user experience, adding one feature at a time. However, in terms of product design, all these standalone pages should be integrated into the dashboard, creating a seamless experience and real powerful product.

CodementorX is now rebanded as Arc.dev. At the time I left the company they just started to renovate its identity (logo, landing page, and name, etc). I believe they will soon start working on revamping the hiring process and improving the user experience inside the product.
